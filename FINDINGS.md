# Key Findings

Green, S. A., Smith, B., & Mathur, M. (2025). Meaningfully reducing consumption of meat and animal products is an unsolved problem: A meta-analysis. *Appetite*, 108233. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.appet.2025.108233

## Scope

- 35 papers, 41 studies, 112 interventions, ~87,000 participants
- Included only randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
- Interventions categorized as: choice architecture, persuasion (health/environmental/animal welfare), psychology (social norms, implementation intentions, etc.), and combined persuasion+psychology

## Main Results

**Overall pooled effect (all MAP):** SMD = 0.07 (95% CI: 0.02–0.12)
Only ~8% of true effects exceeded SMD = 0.2 (a conventional small-effect threshold).

**After publication bias correction:** SMD ≈ 0.01 (95% CI: −0.014 to 0.033) — not distinguishable from zero.

**By persuasion message type:**
- Environmental: SMD = 0.09
- Health: SMD = 0.08
- Animal welfare: SMD = 0.03

**Red and processed meat (RPM) only:** SMD = 0.25 (95% CI: 0.11–0.38); ~48% of effects exceeded SMD = 0.2. Caveat: may reflect substitution to other animal products rather than overall reduction.

**Regional variation:** North American studies showed smaller effects (SMD = 0.04) than studies elsewhere (SMD = 0.14–0.21).

**Methodological strictness:** Relaxing inclusion criteria to match prior reviews (adding 22 marginal studies) raises the pooled estimate to SMD = 0.20, explaining why earlier meta-analyses reported more promising results.

## Conclusion

Meaningfully reducing total MAP consumption via current intervention approaches is an unsolved problem. The authors call for rigorous evaluation of understudied strategies including extended animal contact, price manipulations, disgust activation, and additional choice architecture designs.
